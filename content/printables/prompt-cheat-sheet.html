The Prompting Cheat Sheet

Your prompt is not a wish. It is a brief. The better the brief, the less your AI has to guess.

The core move: talk to AI like a smart new coworker who joined 12 minutes ago. It can move fast, but it needs the assignment, the context, the standard, and the format.

The BRIEF Formula

BBackground

What is happening? What should it know?

RRole + Reader

Who should it act as? Who is this for?

IInstructions

What exactly should it do?

EExamples + Edges

What does good look like? What is out of bounds?

FFormat

What should the answer look like?

Copy-Paste Starter

I need help with [task] for [audience]. Background: [facts, situation, stakes]. Source material: [paste notes, draft, policy, email, transcript, data]. Act as: [role or lens]. Tone: [plainspoken, polished, warm, executive, funny but not chaotic]. Constraints: [length, must include, must avoid, deadline, geography, policy limits]. Format: [table, bullets, memo, script, email, checklist]. Before drafting, ask me up to [number] clarifying questions if anything important is missing.

Why It Works

  • Background reduces generic answers.
  • Role + reader changes what the AI prioritizes.
  • Instructions stop it from wandering.
  • Examples show taste, quality, and pattern.
  • Format makes the output usable immediately.

The 8-Step Prompt Glow-Up

Step by step
  1. Name the job. Summarize the task in one plain sentence.
  2. Name the audience. Executive, client, team, newsletter reader, five-minute learner, skeptical stakeholder.
  3. Paste the receipts. Give the actual email, policy, notes, transcript, numbers, or links the answer should use.
  4. Set the standard. Say what good looks like: concise, practical, low-jargon, legally careful, board-ready, funny but still useful.
  5. Set the boundaries. Include must-use, must-avoid, length, location, confidentiality, and deadline constraints.
  6. Ask for the shape. Tell it whether you want a table, checklist, memo, draft email, script, decision tree, or options.
  7. Make it inspect itself. Ask for assumptions, missing information, risks, or a constraint checklist.
  8. Try it on. Iterate: shorter, warmer, more specific, more senior, more skeptical, more actionable.
The fitting room rule

The first answer is not the outfit. It is what the AI brought into the fitting room.

Try prompts like:

  • "Make this 30 percent shorter and keep only the parts a VP would care about."
  • "Give me three versions: warm, direct, and extremely concise."
  • "What assumptions did you make? Which ones should I verify?"
  • "Turn this into a checklist I can use before sending."
  • "Point out anything vague, risky, or unsupported."

Bad vs Better

Weak Prompt Why It Misses Better Prompt
"Write a LinkedIn post about AI." No audience, voice, point, or outcome. "Write a 140-word LinkedIn post for busy corporate women who are curious about AI but overwhelmed. Point: AI makes more sense when you build something small. Voice: smart friend at work, practical, lightly funny, no hype."
"Summarize this." Summary for whom? What decisions matter? "Summarize this for a senior leader who has 3 minutes. Include: decision needed, top 3 risks, open questions, and a recommendation. Use bullets."
"Make this better." "Better" is a taste test with no recipe. "Edit this email so it is warmer, shorter, and clearer. Keep the ask direct. Do not add facts. Show the final version only."

Prompt Recipes for Real Work

Email Rewrite

Rewrite this email so it sounds [tone] and is easy to act on. Keep the main ask clear, remove filler, and do not invent facts. Give me: 1. Subject line 2. Final email 3. One sentence explaining what changed Draft: [paste]

Executive Summary

Summarize the material below for [audience]. Focus on decisions, risks, and next steps. Use this format: - Bottom line - What changed - Why it matters - Risks or open questions - Recommended next action Material: [paste]

Meeting Prep

Help me prepare for a meeting with [person/team] about [topic]. Use the context below to create: 1. A 30-second opener 2. 5 questions to ask 3. Likely objections 4. Responses that are direct but collaborative 5. A follow-up email template Context: [paste]

Pressure Test

Act as a skeptical but fair reviewer. Pressure-test this plan. Identify: - Weak assumptions - Missing stakeholders - Practical risks - Places where the wording is too vague - 3 changes that would make this stronger Plan: [paste]

Learning Buddy

Teach me [topic] like I am smart but new to it. Start with the plain-English version, then give me an analogy, then a practical example, then 5 questions to check whether I understand.

Prompt Makeover

Improve this prompt. Keep my intent, but make it clearer and more likely to produce a useful answer. Explain what you changed in plain language. Prompt: [paste]

Troubleshooting

Problem Likely Cause Fix Prompt
It sounds generic. Not enough audience, stakes, or taste. "Make this specific to [audience]. Use the facts I gave you. Remove generic AI/business language."
It is too long. No length or priority limit. "Cut this to [word count]. Keep only the points needed for [decision/action]."
The tone is wrong. Tone adjectives are too vague. "Make it sound like [example]. Avoid [words/phrases]. It should feel [3 traits]."
It invented details. The AI filled gaps instead of asking. "Use only the source material below. If something is missing, write 'not provided' and ask a follow-up question."
It missed a constraint. The constraint was buried or not checked. "Revise using this checklist: [constraints]. After the answer, confirm each constraint was met."
It feels too confident. No uncertainty instruction. "Label assumptions, uncertainty, and anything that needs verification before I rely on it."
It is not strategic enough. The role/lens is too junior. "Rework this as if advising a senior leader. Focus on tradeoffs, sequencing, risks, and decision points."

Receipts Rule

  • Verify names, dates, titles, quotes, prices, links, laws, policies, and numbers.
  • For legal, tax, medical, financial, HR, privacy, or safety topics: treat AI as a drafting and thinking tool, not the final authority.
  • Ask for sources, but still check them yourself.
  • Do not paste confidential or sensitive information into tools that are not approved for that use.

Analogy Bank

  • Smart new hire: fast, useful, but needs the assignment and the office context.
  • Clueless closet: AI can pull options, but you still decide what works for the occasion.
  • David Rose specificity: "very fancy" is not enough. Say the occasion, the guest list, and the vibe.
  • Fitting room: the first draft is a try-on, not a commitment.
  • Receipts: for anything important, make the answer show what it is based on.

The 5-Minute Thursday Try-On

Pick one real work task. Write a messy prompt. Then upgrade it with BRIEF. Ask the AI to show what changed. Save the better prompt in your personal prompt closet.
