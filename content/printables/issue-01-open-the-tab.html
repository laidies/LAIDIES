Open the Tab

You do not need a 40-hour course or a new personality. You need one safe first rep.

The core move: AI is not Google in a blazer. It can draft, summarize, compare, translate tone, and turn messy work into a usable first pass. It still needs your context, judgment, and review.

The TAB Method

TTry One Task

Pick something low-risk, real, and small enough to finish in 10 minutes.

AAsk All Three

Run the same prompt in ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini.

BBring Judgment

Compare usefulness, tone, accuracy, and what still needs a human.

Your First Safe Prompt

Explain [topic] to me like I am smart but new to it. Context: I need this for [meeting/email/project/conversation]. Keep it practical and work-appropriate. Give me: 1. The plain-English version 2. One simple example 3. What people often misunderstand 4. Three questions I should ask next Do not assume I know technical terms.

Good First Tasks

  • Explain a concept before a meeting.
  • Summarize a non-confidential article.
  • Rewrite a low-stakes email draft.
  • Turn messy notes into a checklist.
  • Brainstorm questions for a conversation.
  • Compare three possible next steps.

The 10-Minute AI Try-On

Do this once
  1. Choose a low-risk topic. Nothing confidential, sensitive, personal, legal, medical, tax-advice-specific, or unreleased.
  2. Open three tools. ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini.
  3. Paste the same prompt. Do not tweak yet. You are comparing the tools, not your editing skills.
  4. Read like a reviewer. Which answer is clear? Which is useful? Which sounds confident but thin?
  5. Ask one follow-up. Try: "Make this more practical for my situation."
  6. Save the winner. Keep the prompt and the best output in your personal prompt closet.
The butterfly clip rating system
  • 0: Ouch, you stepped on a broken butterfly clip.
  • 10: Amazing, you almost have enough butterfly clips for a full hairstyle.
  • Useful: I could actually use part of this.
  • Clear: I understood it without rereading five times.
  • Specific: It used my context instead of giving generic advice.
  • Accurate enough to keep exploring: no obvious nonsense.
  • Human still needed: I know what I would check or change.

AI Is Not Google in a Blazer

Search Is Usually For

  • Finding a specific page, source, or fact.
  • Checking a current price, rule, date, or event.
  • Opening an official source.
  • Looking up something you can verify directly.

Generative AI Is Usually For

  • Drafting a first pass.
  • Summarizing material you provide.
  • Explaining something in a different way.
  • Comparing options and tradeoffs.
  • Turning messy notes into structure.

Do / Do Not

Do Do Not
Use a real but low-stakes task. Start with confidential, sensitive, or regulated information.
Give context and say what you need the answer for. Ask vague questions and then decide AI is useless.
Compare outputs and keep your judgment. Treat the first answer as finished work.
Ask follow-ups when the answer is close but not useful yet. Assume confident wording means correct wording.
Save prompts that worked. Start from scratch every time.

The Human Job

Your job is taste, context, judgment, and consequences. AI can help with the blank page. It cannot own your standards.

Save This Line

Troubleshooting Your First Rep

Problem Likely Cause Try This
"It gave me fluffy nonsense." Your prompt had no context or standard. "Use my situation below. Make this practical for [audience] and give examples."
"I do not know what to ask." The task is too broad. "Ask me five questions that would help you help me with [topic]."
"The answer sounds wrong." It may be guessing or oversimplifying. "List what you are assuming and what I should verify before relying on this."
"This feels risky for work." The material may be sensitive or confidential. Stop. Remove sensitive details or use only approved tools and approved data.
"I got three different answers." Different models make different choices. Compare the outputs, ask for sources where needed, and use your judgment.

Receipts Rule

  • Verify current facts, names, dates, numbers, laws, policies, quotes, and links.
  • Do not paste confidential work information into tools that are not approved.
  • For tax, legal, HR, medical, financial, privacy, or safety topics, use AI for drafting and thinking, not final authority.
  • When it matters, ask: "What should I verify before relying on this?"
