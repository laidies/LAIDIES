## Episode Promise

The one in which she types something vague into ChatGPT, gets back the world's most useless paragraph, channels her inner David Rose demanding very specific things, tries again with actual context, and watches AI produce something she'd genuinely send to her VP.

Prompting is not coding. It is delegation.

## Opening

Last week I asked AI to help me draft talking points for a meeting. I got back something so generic it could have been a LinkedIn motivational poster printed on a throw pillow.

The next day I asked it to help me write a project update for my director, told it who my director is, what she cares about, what the project actually does, and what I needed her to approve. It came back with something I barely had to edit.

Same tool. Same Tuesday afternoon. Completely different results.

The variable was me. What I was giving it determined what I got back.

## Tell Me What You Want, What You Really Really Want

Prompting is like the drawing game where one person describes something and the other person draws it based only on the description. "Draw a house" gets you a triangle roof. "Draw the Victorian from Practical Magic with the wraparound porch, widow's walk, and overgrown garden" gives the other person enough to make something recognizable.

AI works the same way. It can execute the ask, but it cannot read your mind, does not know your meetings, and will not always stop to ask a clarifying question.

The Spice Girls had the lesson: tell it what you want, what you really really want.

## The David Rose School of Prompting

David Rose would be brilliant at prompting because he is specific. The move is not fancy language, secret formatting, or technical vocabulary. The move is explaining the task, audience, context, tone, constraints, and what "good" looks like.

If you type "write me an email about the project," AI is standing in the kitchen hearing "fold in the cheese." It may try, but you have not told it what that means.

Talk to AI the way you would brief a smart new hire on their first week:

- Who is this for?
- What do they care about?
- What is the tone?
- What does success look like?
- How long should this be?
- What should it not include?
- Is there an example it should follow?

## Same Task, Better Ask

Vague prompt:

```text
Summarize this policy change for my stakeholders.
```

Likely output:

```text
The policy change introduces new requirements for compliance reporting. Key stakeholders should be aware of updated timelines and documentation requirements...
```

Specific prompt:

```text
I need to summarize a policy change for 6 senior managers who have 2 minutes to read this before a meeting. They care about: what's actually changing, when it takes effect, what their teams need to do differently, and whether it affects budget. They don't care about the history of why this changed or the regulatory background. Tone: direct, no fluff, bullet points are fine. 150 words max.
```

That second prompt has a much better chance of producing something usable because it gives AI context and constraints.

## Research Note

Harvard and BCG studied 758 consultants using ChatGPT-4. For tasks inside AI's "jagged frontier," consultants using AI completed work over 25% faster and produced output rated over 40% higher quality. For tasks outside that frontier, some users did worse because they trusted polished wrong answers.

The lesson is not "AI always improves everything." The lesson is that context, judgment, and verification matter.

## So You Don't Pull a Cher

Prompt: what you type into the chat box. It is not code or a spell. It is you being clear about who the work is for, what you need, and what "good" looks like.

Context: the background information that makes AI understand your situation instead of the generic version.

Token: a chunk of text the model processes. Readers do not need to obsess over tokens yet; they just need to know that more context has a cost and tools have limits.
