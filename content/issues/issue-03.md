## Episode Promise

The one in which AI says something with full Regina George confidence, she almost believes it, then remembers that a polished paragraph is not the same thing as a receipt.

Issue 1 was opening the tab.

Issue 2 was learning to ask better.

Issue 3 is learning not to confuse fluent with true.

## Opening

At some point, everyone has the same AI moment: you ask a normal question, the answer looks clean, the tone is calm, the bullets are organized, and it sounds like someone who knows what they are talking about.

Then you click the link, check the date, search the quote, or open the policy, and suddenly the answer is giving Burn Book energy.

It is not always malicious or useless, but it is absolutely not ready to forward.

This is the week we stop treating confidence as proof.

## What A Hallucination Actually Is

An AI hallucination is when a model gives you information that sounds plausible but is wrong, unsupported, outdated, misattributed, or made up.

The important part is not that AI is "lying" in the human sense. The tool is generating likely text based on patterns. It is not automatically checking a database of truth before every sentence. Some tools can browse, search, cite, or retrieve documents, but even then you still need to check whether the source is real and whether it says what the answer claims.

That is the trap: the output can be beautifully written and still be factually shaky.

Polish is style.

Receipts are evidence.

## Why It Feels So Convincing

AI is very good at sounding like the kind of person who has the answer.

It can write in executive tone. It can organize a mess. It can summarize with confidence. It can make a half-formed thought look like it came from a slide deck with a budget.

That is useful when you need structure.

It is risky when you need truth.

Think of AI like a very fast intern who is excellent at formatting, has read everything and nothing, and occasionally says "my cousin went to another school" when asked for proof. You can absolutely work with that intern. You just do not send their first draft to the VP without checking the claims.

## The Receipts Checklist

Before you use AI output at work, check anything that could embarrass you, cost money, mislead a customer, or create a compliance problem.

Start with these receipt zones:

- Names
- Dates
- Numbers
- Links
- Quotes
- Laws, policies, or regulations
- Product names and model names
- Medical, legal, tax, HR, privacy, or financial guidance
- Claims about what a company, customer, or leader said

If the answer includes one of those, do not just admire the formatting. Open the source.

## The Three-Layer Check

Layer 1: Ask AI to show its work.

Use a prompt like:

```text
Before I use this, separate the answer into three sections:
1. Claims you are confident about
2. Assumptions you made
3. Claims I should verify with a primary source
```

Layer 2: Check the actual source.

Do not stop at a citation; open it, confirm the link works, confirm the date, confirm the quoted language is really there, and confirm the source is relevant to the exact question you asked.

Layer 3: Decide whether the stakes require a human expert.

If the topic touches legal, HR, finance, privacy, medical advice, customer commitments, security, or compliance, AI can help you prepare questions. It should not be the final authority.

## Same Output, Better Standard

Weak move:

```text
AI said this vendor policy changed, so I am adding it to the deck.
```

Better move:

```text
AI flagged a possible vendor policy change. I checked the vendor page, confirmed the date, and linked the source in the speaker notes.
```

The second version is not slower because you are bad at AI.

It is stronger because you are using AI like a grown-up tool, not a magic eight ball with lip gloss.

## What To Trust AI For

Trust it for:

- drafting a structure
- making a checklist
- creating comparison questions
- translating jargon into plain English
- spotting what might need verification
- helping you prepare before you read the source

Do not blindly trust it for:

- exact facts
- current prices or deadlines
- legal, tax, medical, HR, privacy, or compliance advice
- quotes and citations
- anything you would be uncomfortable defending in a meeting

The goal is not to become suspicious of everything. The goal is to know where judgment belongs.

## The Try-On

Pick one AI answer you might actually use at work.

Ask it to identify the claims that need verification.

Then check at least three things yourself:

- one name, date, number, link, or quote
- one source it gave you
- one claim that would matter if it were wrong

Post the suspicious part, the corrected version, or the source-check lesson in the Burn Book.

Main Character Energy:

```text
If the output has no receipts, I am not putting my name on it.
```
