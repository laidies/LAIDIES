# Style And Creative Direction

## North Star

lAIdies should feel like a smart friend at work who can explain AI clearly, make the group chat laugh, and still get the deck out by 4 p.m.

The full Season 1 continuity lives in `docs/season/24-episode-arc.md`. Use that before making creative calls for a weekly issue.

## Intelligence And Joy Guardrail

lAIdies must never come across as "women need silly references to learn technical things." The brand belief is stronger and more respectful:

Smart, capable professionals deserve AI learning that is rigorous enough to use at work, clear enough to act on quickly, and fun enough that they actually want to keep going.

90s/Y2K references, games, cards, songs, and jokes are used for memory, momentum, taste, and community. They are not used to dilute the material, talk down to readers, or hide shallow explanations behind nostalgia.

Before publishing, check:

- Did we teach the real concept accurately?
- Did the reference make the idea easier to remember or use?
- Would a senior professional feel respected sharing this with a colleague?
- Does the fun raise the experience, rather than replace the substance?

The creative system is strongest when every piece does three things:

- teaches one useful AI/work concept
- gives the reader a tiny action
- makes the learning feel socially safe and culturally familiar

## Editorial Style

Use the article for the lesson and the website for the activity.

- Newsletter issue: story, analogy, practical explanation, try-on, sign-off.
- Website: quiz, card pack, glossary, Hot Goss, community thread, playlist, archives.
- Social: one saveable idea per post, not the whole issue compressed into a caption.

Avoid making the newsletter carry everything. The issue should not include stale news blocks, large interactive explanations, or every resource. Link out to the site.

## Visual Direction

Keep the Y2K/90s references specific, but keep the interface grown-up enough for a corporate woman to send it to a colleague.

## Exacting Design Standard

Do not be agreeable by default during design work. Be exacting.

Before showing Ali a visual asset, solve the whole design system:

- where the asset will appear
- what surrounding elements will overlap or compete
- how the logo, typography, color, imagery, and negative space work together
- whether the result looks like a finished brand asset, not a mock or template
- whether a seasoned brand designer or marketing leader would consider it polished

Do not show multiple mediocre options. Iterate privately, reject weak attempts, and present the strongest recommendation with a short design rationale.

Good signals:

- glossy trading cards
- magazine quiz energy
- office objects with pop-culture styling
- phone, binder, closet, Rolodex, mix CD, receipt, and card-pack metaphors
- bitmap hero images and module art

Watch-outs:

- too many modules visible at once
- too much pink without contrast
- jokes that are nostalgic but do not teach anything
- decorative assets that do not reveal the actual product, game, or issue
- generic AI sparkle language

## Layout Guidance

The homepage should stay guided, not become a full inventory.

Preferred order:

1. Hero
2. Current episode
3. Community
4. Learn
5. Quiz/card pack/games
6. Resources and references
7. Subscribe
8. Origin story

For issue pages, keep the article readable first. Add one clear website link block near the end for the weekly modules.

## Issue 2 Specific Direction

Issue 2 is the specificity issue, so every creative element should model specificity:

- Link text should say the actual action, not "click here."
- Quiz questions should reinforce context, audience, constraints, and iteration.
- Card rewards should name the behavior being reinforced.
- Hot Goss should stay on the website because news expires faster than the core lesson.

## Creative Bets To Keep

- Card packs are a strong retention mechanic. Keep adding 3-5 cards per issue.
- The quiz archive gives late readers a reason to start from Issue 1.
- "The Try-On" should be the weekly behavioral loop.
- The lAIdies Room is intended to be a live chat/community experience, not a generic contact form. If the live room is not wired yet, treat that as an implementation gap, not a brand retreat.
- The Reference Closet should stay useful, not just nostalgic.
- Madame CLAI-O and Dream Phone are high-personality modules; keep them playful but do not let them crowd the weekly learning path.
- The butterfly clip rating scale is a strong brand mechanic: broken butterfly clip for "ouch," 1-10 clips for feedback, and 10 clips as "almost enough for a full hairstyle."
- "TLDR. But make it fetch." is the preferred frame for website-only news explainers when Hot Goss needs more substance than a link list.
- The Terminal/PowerShell teaching analogy is the secret Starbucks drink menu: apps are the printed menu, commands are the off-menu order that works when you know how to ask.
